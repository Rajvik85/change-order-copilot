# Domain Primer: Change, Claims, and Commercial Control

## The commercial problem

An EPC contract converts scope, time, quality, and risk allocation into a
price. A change order records a controlled departure from that baseline. It may
add or delete scope, respond to a changed design, value an instructed
acceleration, recognize a physical condition, or preserve time and cost
consequences of an external event.

A good CO is not merely an estimate. It connects five propositions:

1. an event occurred;
2. the contract allocates that event to a party;
3. the claimant followed the required procedure;
4. the event caused measurable cost or critical delay; and
5. the valuation follows the contract's rate hierarchy and evidence rules.

The Copilot mirrors those propositions. Extraction establishes facts;
compliance tests procedure and clause alignment; analytics identifies portfolio
patterns. It does not determine legal entitlement.

## Why notice discipline matters

Notice clauses give the receiving party a fair chance to investigate,
mitigate, keep records, and make budget decisions while the facts are fresh. A
time bar makes that procedure a condition to entitlement. In the synthetic
contract, initial notice is due within 14 calendar days after awareness and
detailed particulars within 28.

Professionals lose otherwise arguable money when they:

- wait for the cost to become certain before notifying the event;
- treat meeting minutes as notice without identifying contractual effect;
- date a submission but cannot prove service;
- notify a continuing impact but never update particulars;
- merge several events into one retrospective claim; or
- debate quantum before preserving procedural rights.

The engine therefore distinguishes three positions:

- **● COMPLIANT:** both dates exist and the initial notice is within the limit.
- **▲ AT RISK:** the dates needed to prove timeliness are missing.
- **■ TIME-BARRED:** elapsed calendar days exceed the configured period.

It shows the subtraction. “Late” is never an opaque label.

## Change types and commercial evidence

### Scope addition

The instruction, scope boundary, quantity take-off, rate derivation, and
schedule interface matter. A lump sum without build-up may be commercially
possible but weak for review.

### Design change

The central question is baseline versus change. Normal design development and
correction of contractor error are not automatically variations. Freeze
records, approved deliverables, comment timing, and rework traceability matter.

### Differing site conditions

The comparison is not “expected versus difficult”; it is tender information and
reasonable foreseeability versus encountered fact. Boreholes, daily reports,
photographs, survey levels, plant records, and disposal tickets carry more
weight than retrospective narrative.

### Acceleration

Acceleration should identify an instruction, milestone, incremental resources,
premium time, productivity assumptions, and recovery fragnet. Paying overtime
without checking whether it actually recovers the critical path buys cost, not
necessarily time.

### Prolongation and EOT

Time entitlement and money entitlement are related but different. A critical
Employer-risk delay may justify time; compensable prolongation requires
demonstrable time-related cost and concurrency analysis. Claimed days are not
summed across COs as if each day were independently critical.

### Force majeure / exceptional event

The event must fit the invented allocation, be outside control, and show
avoidance and mitigation. Time and cost consequences may differ. “Force
majeure” is a characterization, not evidence.

### Deletion and descope

Credits should include avoided direct and indirect cost, adjusted for evidenced
cancellation charges. Descope is a commercial offset; portfolios that show
only additions overstate exposure.

### Backcharge

The reviewer looks for defect or non-performance, cure notice, reasonable
opportunity to correct, causal scope, and evidenced third-party cost. A
backcharge is not self-proving because an invoice exists.

## Cumulative impact

Small instructions can share supervision, workfront congestion, resequencing,
and learning loss. Assessing each in isolation may miss interaction; adding
every percentage may double count it. The product flags repeated sub-USD 250k
COs from one contractor as a review trigger, not as a calculated disruption
entitlement.

## Commercial “games” and their controls

Nineteen years of project controls experience teaches that weak behavior is
usually procedural before it becomes analytical:

| Behavior | Commercial risk | Copilot response |
|---|---|---|
| Padding contingency into every CO | Duplicated risk and poor settlement signal | Cost-breakdown finding and gross/net views |
| Retrospective basis-hunting | Narrative precedes contractual route | Clause-to-type alignment check |
| Aggregating unrelated events | Causation and time-bar dates become blurred | One canonical event/notice pair per CO |
| Ignoring descope credits | Exposure is overstated | Signed values and net/gross analytics |
| Claiming days without a fragnet | No proof of critical effect | Schedule-evidence finding |
| Splitting changes below authority | Governance bypass | Cumulative contractor analytics |
| Treating silence as timely notice | Evidence gap becomes assumed compliance | AT RISK, never PASS |
| Settling quantum before procedure | Negotiating the wrong issue first | Critical time-bar finding ranks first |

These are screening rules. A competent reviewer still reads the instruction,
contract, records, estimate, and schedule.
